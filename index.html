Premier League Player Transfers

"The moneyball approach doesn't really work with football in the same way it does with baseball because of the game's free flowing nature."

The moneyball approach, an analytical, evidence-based approach to predicting a player's abilities, doesn't really work with football in the same way it does with baseball because of the game's free flowing nature. One of the reasons being touted is that there's two much chaos in the pattern of play to pick up players on secondary or unconventional performance stats. Couple this with the vast difference in playing styles, climate and pitches across different leagues in defferent nations, or even playing styles of different teams within the same league, make it almost impossible to use a purely statistical approach to cater to a team's needs in the transfer market.

The Premier League, arguably the biggest league in Football in terms of revenue, consistently dishes out large sums of money to sign players from all over Europe and the rest of the world. However, not all of their signings are able to deliver. Notable examples include Juan Sebastian Veron and Angel Di Maria to Manchester United, Andriy Shevchenko and Fernando Torres to Chelsea, Andy Carrol to Liverpool, Robinho, Jovetic and a host of other players to Manchester City.

Team transfer activity

In this project I have accumulated transfer activity data for some of the bigger clubs in the Premier League, specifically the annual net expenditure on players and the net amount earned by selling players to other clubs. I have viusalized this data and compared it with the clubs' posistion in the league in order to find if there can be any direct relationships. I have also included other significant events in the club during this period of time such as change of managers, achievement in european or other domestic competitions, change in club hierarchy or ownership etc.

The most significant observation is the large amount of spending that invariably follows a takeover of a club. It is also seen that appointing a new manager usually entails significant amount of transfer activity. However, there is no direct evidence to suggest that massive spending has a significant impact on the team's performance in the league. This is apparent from the performances of Claudio Ranieri's Chelsea in the 2003-04 season, Manchester City during the season through 2007-10, Andre Villas Boas's Chelsea in the 2011-12 season and Louis Van Gaal's Manchester United in the 2014-15 and 2015-16 seasons. A better parameter to understand the reasons affecting team performance would be to look at the players that left the club and the players that were brought in to replace them.

Analysis of player transfers

The formula for a successful team to continue in its success is often to appropriately replace the players in the team who have either retired or moved away to a different club. This can include bringing in new players from other clubs or promoting youth from the academy or a combination of both. Manchester United tends to stand out in this regard by consistently assembling a team that could successfully challenge for the league title across two decades, winning 13 league titles, 2 european cups and numerous other trophies in the process. Very rarely do clubs undergo a major overhaul of their entire squad due to the immense expenditure that assembling a successful team entails. However there are examples of this too. Claudio Ranieri and Chelsea in 2003-04, Brendan Rogers and Liverppol in the 2014-15 season, Louis Van Gaal and Manchester United in both his seasons at the club and Manchester City almost every other season.
